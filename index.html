Abstract

Unmanned aerial vehicles are widely used in many different areas, but their operation is still limited by short flight time. This has led researchers to explore solar power as a method to sustain UAV flight for longer durations. By placing lightweight solar cells on the wings or fuselage, UAVs can harvest sunlight during flight and convert it into electrical energy. Recent advancements in photovoltaic efficiency, lighter materials, and energy management systems have made long-endurance solar UAVs increasingly feasible. Engineers are now developing more aerodynamically efficient wings, reducing structural weight, and planning flight paths that optimise exposure to sunlight. These developments suggest that solar-powered UAVs may soon become practical for applications requiring long and continuous operation.

Introduction

Unmanned Aerial Vehicles are gaining popularity in environmental monitoring, emergency response, and communication support, but their usefulness is still restricted by short battery life (Li et al., 2024). Solar power offers a renewable and continuous energy source, enabling longer flight durations (El-Atab et al., 2021), (Sornek et al., 2025). Solar-powered UAVs can generate electricity to run motors or recharge onboard batteries, significantly increasing endurance (Abdulrahman et al., 2025). Improvements in photovoltaic efficiency, lighter structures, and energy management have further enhanced long-endurance capabilities (Bakar et al., 2021). Strategies such as storing excess daytime energy or adjusting altitude profiles also support extended missions (Gao et al., 2023).

Wing Design and Structural Considerations

Wing design plays a central role in improving endurance of solar UAVs (Panagiotou & Yakinthos, 2020). Long slender wings reduce drag and increase aerodynamic efficiency, supporting flight at lower power inputs (Bakar et al., 2021). Lightweight composite materials, such as carbon fibre, provide structural strength while keeping mass low (Cipolla et al., 2023). However, thin wings may experience aeroelastic deformation, affecting stability and solar panel orientation, making structural modelling essential (Cipolla et al., 2023).

Energy Generation and Management Strategies

Photovoltaic cells convert sunlight into electrical energy, which powers the UAV or charges batteries for later use (Gao et al., 2023). Energy availability varies throughout the day, requiring effective energy storage and distribution methods. One common strategy involves climbing to higher altitudes during the day using abundant solar energy and gliding at night to conserve battery power (Gao et al., 2023). Advanced energy management systems ensure efficient distribution between propulsion, avionics, and communication equipment, improving reliability during long missions.

Design Optimisation for Long-Endurance Missions

Optimisation techniques are essential to designing UAVs capable of long-endurance missions. Computational tools test combinations of wing geometry, solar panel placement, battery capacity, and structural materials before physical construction. Genetic algorithms help identify configurations that maximise endurance (Bakar et al., 2021). Multidisciplinary design optimisation integrates aerodynamics, structures, and energy systems to evaluate how changes in one subsystem affect overall performance (Li et al., 2020).

Conclusion

Solar-powered UAVs show strong potential for overcoming the endurance limits of traditional battery-powered drones. With efficient wing designs, lightweight materials, advanced energy-management systems, and optimisation techniques, researchers are bringing long-duration autonomous flight closer to practical reality. These developments support future applications in environmental monitoring, remote communication, and sustainable aviation.
